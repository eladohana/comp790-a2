US House of Representatives Representation of voters

Premise:

U.S. Elections are based on a winner-take-all system where members of the House of Representatives are selected by districts divided along geographical lines.

Each district selects from a pool of candidates, where the one with the most votes is chosen to represent that district.

This setup, however, could lead to scenarios where voters are not well represented.

For instance, consider that a candidate can win their district with over 50% of the votes in their district. Now, consider the extreme, though unlikely, scenario where 51% of districts elect candidates from a single party, each winning with 51% of the votes, and that all other voters choose the opposing party. This now leads the majority party to have won with only about 26% of the votes!

While this scenario is very improbable, it is important to ask how well do members of congress represent their constituents?

In all datasets, the data was modified to remove special elections and elections superceded by runoffs to avoid over-representing any state.

With the exception of the last graphics, only Democrat and Republican parties are included, since other parties do not have significant representation, either in representatives or voters.

Representation by state

The graphic below shows congressional maps of the contiguous states based on party leaning from 1980, 2000, and 2020.

It was created using Tableau.

While the maps are useful to see the leanings based on geographic locations, it would be very difficult to clearly show more data.

To address this, we created a table for each state at every election cycle from 1976–2022

The following attributes:

color: Democrat (blue), Republican (red)

size: voter alignment with representatives (bigger means voters and representatives match more closely)

mouse-over tooltip: State (year), congressional party lean (total members), percentage voter lean.

This was created using d3

We tend to see that states with less members tend to be less well-represented. This is expected since any shift in elected representatives will have a high impact on the percentage.

National Results

Finally, we look at combined candidates and voters throughout the country to see if state inconsistencies are averaged out.

The graphic below shows a percentage stack of congressional winners and the voters for all parties from 1976 to 2022.

The charts were created in altair

Here we tend to see that voters have mostly stayed in the middle, where congressional makeup has mostly followed, with slightly larger spikes at certain times.

A possible conclusion could be that while local results may sometimes be skewed, on a national level, congress tends to stay close to voter makeup despite the possibility of edge cases that the system may allow.
